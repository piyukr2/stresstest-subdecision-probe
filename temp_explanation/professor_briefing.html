A Professor's Walk-Through of Your Independent Study

Sub-decision prosodic sensitivity in speech-LLMs — the question, the method, the controls, the numbers.

PART 1The Big Question

1.1 The everyday phenomenon

Say the sentence "I never said he stole the money" out loud, five times, each time hammering a different word:

Stressed versionWhat it implies
I never said he stole the money…but somebody else did
I never said he stole the money…I deny ever saying it
I never said he stole the money…I implied it, didn't say it
I never said he stole the money…somebody else stole it
I never said he stole the money…he borrowed it, maybe
I never said he stole the money…he stole something else

Same words. Same grammar. Six different meanings. The difference lives entirely in prosody — which syllables get louder, longer, higher in pitch. Humans do this effortlessly. The question is whether machines that "listen" can.

1.2 What a "speech-LLM" is

A normal large language model reads text and predicts the next token. A speech-LLM (e.g. Qwen2-Audio) is the same idea but with an audio encoder bolted on the front: raw waveform → audio features → fed into the LLM alongside the text prompt.

   ┌────────────┐    ┌──────────────────┐    ┌──────────────────┐
   │  Waveform  │──▶│  Audio encoder    │──▶│                  │
   └────────────┘    │  (Whisper-ish)    │    │                  │
                     └──────────────────┘    │   Language model │──▶ next token
   ┌────────────┐                            │   (transformer)  │   (the answer)
   │ Text prompt│──────────────────────────▶│                  │
   └────────────┘                            └──────────────────┘

1.3 The known finding — and the gap your project fills

The StressTest benchmark (HUJI, 2025) built exactly the example above into 218 audio clips: same sentence, different prosodic stress, two candidate meanings, one correct. Current speech-LLMs score about 50% — chance. As far as the "final answer" goes, they look deaf to stress.

The gap

"Final answer" is a coarse measurement. A model can be 51% sure of A and 49% sure of B — that's a wrong final answer, but the internal distribution may still be shifting in the right direction when the stress changes. That sub-decision shift is what your project measures.

When the model gets the contrastive-stress question wrong, does the probability distribution over the two answer tokens still move in the direction that matches the spoken stress?

If yes → the model hears prosody but can't yet act on it confidently. A real, publishable mechanistic finding even though the headline accuracy looks like chance.

PART 2The Core Mechanism: Reading Logits

The single most important technical concept in the project. Take your time on it.

2.1 What a logit is

A transformer LLM, at every position, produces a vector of size ≈ vocabulary (~150,000 numbers). These are logits. Pass them through softmax and you get a probability distribution over every token.

When you call model.generate(...) you get back a single token — the argmax. That throws away an enormous amount of information.

2.2 The trick: don't call generate, call forward

Give the model the prompt that ends right before the answer slot:

   Out of the following answers, ... 1. ... 2. ... Answer:_
                                                          ▲
                                       prediction lives here

Run model.forward(...) once. Read the logits at the last input position. From that 150k-element vector, pick out just two numbers — the logit for token 1 and the logit for token 2. 2-way softmax them:

   P("1" | audio, prompt)  =  e^L₁ / (e^L₁ + e^L₂)
   P("2" | audio, prompt)  =  e^L₂ / (e^L₁ + e^L₂)

This is Strategy 2 in your CLAUDE.md. Faster than generating, and it gives you the probability magnitude, not just the winner.

2.3 Why this matters for your hypothesis

Two stress versions of the same sentence might give:

Cliplogit("1")logit("2")P("1")argmax
Stress favors meaning 14.24.00.55"1" ✓
Stress favors meaning 24.13.90.55"1" ✗

Both argmaxes are "1" — both look like failures at the top level. But notice: when the stress should push toward meaning 2, the probability still moved. That sub-decision shift is your signal.

2.4 Why the space-prefix obsession in the token IDs

The Qwen tokenizer treats "A" and " A" as different tokens with different IDs. After the prompt Answer:, the next character the model emits is a space — so the predicted token is A (ID 362), not A (ID 322).

The Session 2 bug

Get this wrong and you read the wrong column of the logit vector. Your entire experiment is silently broken. The smoke test caught it: the model was producing "Based" / "The" because the assistant turn was empty.

Lesson: always sanity-check that the token you read the logit for is the token the model actually wants to emit. That's what Strategy 1 (a regular generate call on 20 items) is for.

PART 3Two Answer Formats — Why Both

You don't run the experiment once — you run it twice, with two prompt phrasings:

  • Format A ... A. <meaning1> B. <meaning2> Answer: → predicts A or B
  • Format B ... 1. <meaning1> 2. <meaning2> Answer: (trailing space!) → predicts 1 or 2

Why two? A critic could say: "you cherry-picked a phrasing the model happens to be sensitive to." Replicating in Format B turns that into: "two independent answer-token choices both show the effect." Cheapest possible robustness check; huge credibility return.

3.1 The four possible answer tokens — and why we only read two

When the Format A prompt ends with Answer: (no trailing space), the model's next token could in principle be any of four candidates:

TokenIDWhat it would mean
' A'362space + A — the natural continuation after a colon
' B'425space + B — same
'A'32bare A — would mean "no space," unusual after a colon
'B'33bare B — same

A well-trained LM almost always emits the space-prefixed version after a colon, so the pipeline reads just two logits — at positions 362 and 425 — and ignores 32 / 33.

Why this shortcut is safe

The Strategy 1 ↔ Strategy 2 smoke test (§2.4) is exactly the check for this. Strategy 1 calls model.generate(...) and asks "what token did you actually emit?" If the answer were ever bare 'A' (32) or 'B' (33), the strict-match logic would fail. It passed on all 20 sanity-check items — confirming the model concentrates probability on ' A' / ' B', and reading only two logits costs us nothing.

The principled fallback (not needed here)

If the smoke test had ever caught the model emitting bare 'A' or 'B', the fix would be to read all four logits and combine them by summing the A-pair and the B-pair before softmax:

   P("means A") = softmax([logit_362 + logit_32, logit_425 + logit_33])[0]

We didn't need to. The two-logit read is clean — and that's what makes the smoke test non-negotiable: it's the check that lets us trust the shortcut.

Format B sidesteps the problem differently

The Format B prompt is built to end with "Answer: " (trailing space already there). The natural next token is just '1' (16) or '2' (17) — no leading space needed, because the space was absorbed into the prompt. The candidates ' 1' / ' 2' aren't even single tokens on this tokenizer (you can see this in the verify_tokens.py output, where ' 1' = [220, 16] and ' 2' = [220, 17]), so there's no ambiguity at all for Format B.

Reading verify_tokens.py's "FAILED" line

The script ends with FAILED: ' 1', ' 2' are NOT single tokens. This is expected and harmless — those are precisely the strings we don't use. The four token IDs the pipeline actually relies on (362, 425, 16, 17) all pass round-trip decoding cleanly.

4.1 Text-only

If the transcript alone lets the LLM pick the right answer above chance, then any positive result on the audio path could just be the LLM exploiting wording — not hearing stress.

Result ✓

A = 49.5%, B = 48.2%, both at chance. The transcript does not leak the answer. Control is clean.

4.2 Cascade — and why text-only isn't enough on its own

Pipe the audio through Whisper (an ASR system) to get a transcript, then feed that text to the LLM. Whisper writes down words but it does not write down which word was stressed.

At first this looks redundant with text-only — both end up feeding text to an LLM. But they rule out two different boring explanations.

What each pathway can "see"

Imagine three witnesses describing the same audio clip of "I never said he stole the money" (stress on "he" → meaning: somebody else stole it):

PathwayWhat it getsWhat it can detect
Primary audio Raw waveform "I hear the voice. I hear which word was emphasized."
Text-only Gold transcript only — no audio "I see the sentence as if reading a book. I have no idea which word was loud."
Cascade Audio → Whisper → transcript → LLM "I had access to the audio, but only through a translator who wrote down the words, not the emphasis."

Notice that text-only and cascade end up with the same kind of input: a string of words. So why run both?

The skeptical reviewer attack that text-only can't defend against

Suppose your numbers come out: primary 55%, text-only 49%. A reviewer says:

"Nice — but how do I know the audio model's edge is prosody? Maybe Qwen2-Audio is just a better word-reader. Maybe it caught a faint word the gold transcript got slightly wrong. You've shown that something about audio access helps, not that stress helps."

Pretend the reviewer is right. Imagine the speaker actually mumbled an extra word the dataset transcriber missed:

SourceWhat it contains
Actual audio"I never really said he stole the money."
Gold transcript"I never said he stole the money." (missed "really")

If "really" is the clue that helps pick the right answer, then:

  • Text-only lands at chance — the helpful word is missing from its input.
  • Primary audio scores higher — but not because of prosody. Because of an extra word.

You would see exactly the numbers you got and wrongly conclude "it's prosody!" when it was just a better word read.

Enter cascade

Run cascade. Whisper listens to the same audio and writes down whatever words it hears:

   Whisper transcript: "I never really said he stole the money."

If "really" were the secret clue, cascade would catch it too — because Whisper heard it. So cascade would also score above chance.

What actually happened ✓

Cascade A = 49.5%, B = 48.2% — identical to text-only to three decimals. No hidden extra word. Whisper heard exactly what the gold transcript had. So when the primary audio model still beats both, the only thing left that could be helping is what Whisper threw away when it wrote down only the words — the prosody.

The courtroom analogy

Three witnesses to a party are asked, "did anything unusual happen?"

  • Witness A (text-only): read a written summary afterward. Says no.
  • Witness B (cascade): was at the party, but is deaf and could only lip-read words. Says no.
  • Witness C (primary audio): was at the party with full hearing. Says yes — someone shouted.

Witness A failing tells you the written summary didn't capture it — but maybe the summary just left out words. You can't tell. Witness B also failing tells you something stronger: even being physically present at the party didn't help if you could only catch the words. The unusual thing wasn't in the words. It had to be in something only a hearing person could detect — a shout.

That's the leap cascade lets you make. Text-only alone leaves the door open ("maybe the summary missed words"). Cascade closes it.

One-line summary

Text-only proves: the words don't give the answer away.
Cascade proves: even hearing the audio but only writing down words doesn't give the answer away.

The gap between those two is exactly the slot where "prosody" lives. You need both fences to corner the conclusion.

4.3 The noise floor

How do you know a stress-induced shift is bigger than what you'd get from any random perturbation? You build a noise floor from meaning-preserving perturbations:

PerturbationWhat it doesReal-world analogy
Gain change (−3 dB) Multiplies the whole waveform's amplitude by a constant factor (here, ~0.71×) so the clip is uniformly quieter. Decibels are logarithmic: −3 dB ≈ half the power, but still clearly audible. Turning the volume knob down a notch.
Additive Gaussian noise (~30 dB SNR) Adds random hiss to the waveform. SNR = signal-to-noise ratio; 30 dB means the speech is ~1000× more powerful than the noise — audible hiss but speech remains very clear. A faint hum from a fan in the background.
Time-stretch slow (×0.95) Plays the clip 5% slower without changing pitch. Same words, same melody, same stress pattern — just a slightly longer duration. The speaker talking a touch more slowly.
Time-stretch fast (×1.05) Plays the clip 5% faster, pitch preserved. Same words, same stress, slightly shorter. The speaker talking a touch more quickly.

The key property: none of these change what was said or which word was stressed. A human listener would still hear the same sentence with the same emphasis on the same word. They only rough up the surface of the audio.

Any logit shifts they produce are therefore the model reacting to irrelevant acoustic noise. Those shifts define your floor. For a stress shift to count as a real prosodic effect, it must be larger than the shift the model produces just from these meaning-preserving roughenings.

A note that becomes important in §6.3

Time-stretching turned out to shift Qwen2-Audio's logits ~10× more than gain or noise — even though, to a human, a 5% speed change is barely perceptible. That's a finding about how the model encodes time, and it's what made the pre-registered threshold fail. Hold onto it.

PART 6The Results So Far

Two headline measurements, one transparency note, and one summary table. Read in order.

6.0 About the dataset: 218 clips, 133 pairs, 74 eligible

Before the headline numbers, a short note about how the StressTest dataset is organized — three different totals (218, 133, 74) show up across the analysis, and it's worth being clear on what each one counts.

From clips to pairs

The dataset contains 218 audio clips, but those clips are organized into sentences. Every sentence is recorded with multiple stress placements — 85 sentences have two stress versions and 16 have three:

   85 sentences × 2 stress versions  =  170 clips
   16 sentences × 3 stress versions  =   48 clips
                                       ───────────
                                         218 clips

Now form every within-sentence pair — i.e. pair each version of a sentence with each of its sisters:

   85 sentences × C(2,2) = 1 pair each  =   85 pairs
   16 sentences × C(3,2) = 3 pairs each =   48 pairs
                                          ───────────
                                            133 pairs

So 218 clips → 133 sister pairs. The Wilcoxon test in §6.2 uses one paired observation per item (N = 218); the decision-flip metric in §6.1 starts from these 133 pairs and filters further.

Why only 74 pairs are "eligible" for Headline 1

The decision-flip metric asks: did the model's argmax flip in the correct direction when the stress changed? For that question to be meaningful, the two sister clips must have different gold answers — otherwise there's no flip to make. If both versions of a sentence point to the same correct answer, the pair is dropped.

Filtering 133 pairs down to those with different gold labels leaves 74 eligible pairs. The remaining 59 are "same-meaning" sister pairs — different stress, but the dataset says they map to the same answer.

Where do same-label pairs come from?

Mostly from the 16 three-stress-pattern sentences. With three versions and only two possible answers, the pigeonhole principle forces at least two versions to share a gold answer. Here's a real example from logits_primary_A.jsonl:

FieldValue
Sentence"I didn't take your book."
Possible answers0: "Maybe found or borrowed it."  |  1: "Someone else might have taken it, but not me."
Version A stressI didn't take your book  →  gold = 1
Version B stressI didn't take your book  →  gold = 1

Both stress placements emphasize "it wasn't me" / "I didn't take it" — different focus, but both consistent with the same answer (someone else might have taken it). There's no correct direction to flip in, so this pair contributes to the 133 total but not to the 74 eligible.

Summary

218 = audio clips.   133 = all within-sentence pairs (used by Wilcoxon at the item level).   74 = pairs where the two versions actually have different correct answers (used by the decision-flip rate).

6.1 Headline 1 — Decision-flip rate

What we're measuring

Find every pair of clips in the dataset where the same sentence is spoken with different stress and therefore has different correct answers. (E.g. "I never said he stole it" → answer 1; "I never said he stole it" → answer 2.) There are 74 such pairs.

For each pair, ask: when stress changes from version 1 to version 2, does the model's final answer also flip in the correct direction? If we're listening to a sensitive prosody-aware model, we want to see flips align with the stress.

FormatFlip rate (correct direction)95% bootstrap CI
A9.5%4.1% – 16.2%
B12.2%5.4% – 20.3%

What the number means

If the model were completely deaf to stress, the flip rate would average around 0% (flips would happen randomly in both directions and cancel out). 9.5% / 12.2% means: across all pairs, the net flip is positive — flips that match the stress outnumber flips that contradict it. The rate is modest, but it points the right way.

Why the confidence intervals matter

Both CIs exclude zero. With only 74 pairs, a small positive number alone wouldn't be convincing — it could just be luck. The CI says: even under repeated resampling, the result stays positive. The effect is real, just small.

Aside — what "95% bootstrap CI" actually means

A 95% bootstrap confidence interval is a range that tells you how stable the measurement is — if you had collected slightly different data, where would the number likely land?

For the Format A flip rate (9.5%), here's the recipe:

  1. Start with the 74 eligible pairs and their actual flip rate (9.5%).
  2. Resample: draw a new sample of 74 pairs with replacement from the original 74 (some pairs appear twice, some not at all). Recompute the flip rate on this fake sample.
  3. Repeat step 2 10,000 times. You now have 10,000 slightly different flip rates.
  4. Sort them. The 2.5th percentile and the 97.5th percentile of that list are the lower and upper bounds of the 95% CI — for Format A, 4.1% – 16.2%.

Read it as: "if we re-ran this experiment many times on similar data, the flip rate would fall in this range 95% of the time."

The key check is whether zero falls inside or outside the interval. Here both intervals (4.1–16.2% and 5.4–20.3%) sit fully above zero, so even under unfavorable resampling the effect stays positive — it isn't an artifact of the particular 74 pairs we got. If the lower bound had been negative, we couldn't have ruled out a true rate of zero.

It's called bootstrap because the method "pulls itself up by its bootstraps" — it estimates uncertainty using only the data already in hand, without assuming any particular distribution shape.

Counterfactual — what if 0% had fallen inside the CI?

Worth knowing precisely because the project's main claim doesn't depend on it.

The literal reading

A CI like −2% to +12% straddles zero. The point estimate (say 5%) might look positive, but the data are also consistent with no effect — or with a small effect in the wrong direction. We couldn't rule out that the observed positive number is just noise from having only 74 pairs.

What it would have meant for the project's claims

The decision-flip rate result would have lost its weight. We couldn't write "the model flips its argmax with the stress more often than against it." The strongest defensible sentence would be: "on these 74 pairs we observed a slight positive flip rate, but the 95% CI does not exclude zero."

What would not have been killed

Headline 2 (paired Wilcoxon, p ≈ 10⁻¹⁸) still stands. It asks a different question:

  • Headline 1 asks: does the argmax flip with stress?
  • Headline 2 asks: does the sub-decision logit distribution shift with stress, even when the argmax doesn't flip?

The whole point of the project is that the sub-decision answer can be positive while the argmax looks flat. A null Headline 1 alongside a strong Headline 2 would actually be the most predicted outcome under the project's hypothesis: "the model hears prosody but isn't confident enough to flip its top-1 answer."

Pair-consistency (primary 13.5–19.5% vs text/cascade 8.3%) would also still stand — it uses all 133 pairs and a different baseline.

How the writeup would have framed it

"At the argmax level the effect is too small to detect with 74 pairs (CI includes zero). But below the argmax, where the project's hypothesis actually lives, the effect is overwhelming (p ≈ 10⁻¹⁸). This is the exact picture predicted by 'the model hears stress but doesn't act on it confidently' — and it's why measuring sub-decision shifts mattered in the first place."

A null at the argmax level would have vindicated the choice to look at sub-decision logits, not weakened it.

The actual situation

Both observed CIs (4.1–16.2% and 5.4–20.3%) sit fully above zero, so we get a small but real argmax-level bonus on top of the headline. The point of laying out this counterfactual: even in the worst case the project's main claim survives, because Headline 2 was always the load-bearing result.

6.2 Headline 2 — Paired Wilcoxon

What we're measuring

The decision-flip rate (§6.1) only looks at the final answer. But the project's whole point is that the action is happening below the final answer, in the logit probabilities. So we measure shifts directly.

For each of 218 items, compute two numbers:

  • stress_shift — how much the probability moved between the two stress versions of the sentence (the real experimental signal)
  • floor_shift — how much the probability moved when we applied the strictest meaning-preserving perturbation to the clean clip (the "random jitter" baseline from §4.3)

Then ask, item by item: was the stress shift bigger than the floor shift? If stress is a real signal, the answer should be "yes" on most items.

Why Wilcoxon and not a t-test

The Wilcoxon signed-rank test is the non-parametric paired test for this. We use it because logit shifts are not normally distributed — they're skewed and have heavy tails, which would break a t-test's assumptions. Wilcoxon only assumes the differences are symmetric around their median; it's the safe choice.

Formatp-valuePairs where stress > floor
A4.2 × 10⁻¹⁸163 / 218
B7.7 × 10⁻¹⁷158 / 218

What the number means

A p-value of 10⁻¹⁸ means: if stress had no effect at all and we ran this test on random data, we'd see a result this extreme roughly once in a billion-billion experiments. Practically, this nails the central hypothesis.

Interpretation

Across the dataset, prosodic stress moves the model's internal distribution more than a random meaning-preserving audio perturbation does — by a margin too large to be sampling noise. This is the project's central claim, and it landed.

6.3 The pre-registered threshold FAILED — and why that's interesting

What we said in advance

Before running the experiment, you committed to a stricter bar than Wilcoxon: the median stress shift across items must exceed the 95th percentile of the strictest noise-floor distribution. In plain words: "the typical stress shift must be larger than the worst-case floor shift you'd see by chance."

That stricter bar did not pass in either format.

Why it failed

The culprit

Time-stretch perturbations produced surprisingly large logit shifts — about 10× larger than gain or noise, even at tiny ±2% stretches that humans can barely notice. The 95th percentile of stretch_fast's shift distribution is ~0.30, which is larger than the median stress shift. So the median-stress-vs-95th-floor comparison was always going to lose.

What this actually tells us

This is not "the stress effect isn't real." We already know it's real from the Wilcoxon test, which is the more honest measurement (it compares each item's stress shift to its own floor shift, item by item, paired).

What the threshold failure tells us instead: Qwen2-Audio is unusually fragile to small time-stretches. A 2% speed change perturbs its output as much as a real prosodic difference does. That's a finding about how this model encodes time, and it deserves to be reported as a secondary result.

How to report it

Wilcoxon as the headline. Threshold failure documented openly — not buried — with the time-stretch fragility framed as a model-robustness finding. Pre-registering and then transparently reporting both passes and failures is the gold standard; hiding the failure would be worse than the failure itself.

6.4 Why pair-consistency is the better metric than single-item accuracy

Before reading the all-pathways table below, it helps to spell out what "pair-both-correct" actually measures — because it's the column where the project's story finally becomes visible.

Single-item accuracy hides the answer

Single-item accuracy judges every clip independently: of the 218 clips, how many did the model get right? Each clip is its own coin flip. The problem: a model that just guesses "answer 1" every time scores ~50% — without any understanding of stress.

Pair-consistency uses the dataset's structure

The 218 clips aren't independent — they're recorded as sister pairs: same sentence, different stress, different correct answer. For each of the 133 sister pairs, ask: did the model get both clips of the pair right?

Getting both right is only possible if the model:

  • correctly identified meaning 1 when stress favored meaning 1, and
  • correctly identified meaning 2 when stress favored meaning 2.

That requires actually tracking the stress between the two clips.

Chance × chance — what a random model would score

StrategyPair-both-correct
Independent coin flips (50/50 each clip)0.5 × 0.5 = 25%
"Lazy" model — always guesses the same answer0% (sisters have opposite gold answers by construction)
Text-only / cascade pathway (measured)8.3% — the practical "no-stress baseline"

Why this metric is stricter

Single-item accuracy gives partial credit for half-understanding. Pair-consistency doesn't — you have to nail both members of the pair. The only way to do that consistently is by reading the prosodic difference between them.

Why this matters for the headline story

The single-item accuracies were essentially tied (~50%) across primary, text-only, and cascade — at that level all pathways look the same. But pair-consistency exposes the difference: primary 13.5% / 19.5% vs text/cascade 8.3%. That ~2× lift only appears on the metric that requires actually tracking stress across the pair — which is exactly the information the audio model has and the text pathways don't.

Wait — 8.3% is below the 25% chance baseline. What does that mean?

Sharp question. The naive "25% chance" assumes the two clips of a sister pair are judged independently. They aren't. The errors are forced to be correlated, and that's the whole story.

Text-only and cascade see identical inputs on sister clips

For both clips of a sister pair:

  • Text-only gets the same gold transcript twice (e.g. "I never said he stole it" for both versions).
  • Cascade gets the same Whisper transcript twice — Whisper writes down words, not stress.

A deterministic forward pass on identical inputs gives identical outputs. The model is forced to give the same answer to both clips. It literally cannot tell them apart.

Why that drives pair-both-correct way below 25%

Split the 133 pairs into two groups:

GroupCountWhat happens for text / cascade
Sisters with different gold labels 74 Same answer to both → exactly one right, one wrong → 0% pair-both-correct on this group, guaranteed
Sisters with the same gold label 59 Same answer to both → either both right or both wrong, depending on whether the model lands on the correct shared answer

The 74 different-label pairs are pre-lost — no amount of skill helps. The observed 8.3% (≈ 11 pairs out of 133) all come from the 59 same-label group, where the model happened to land on the correct shared answer.

The three implications
  1. The dataset is adversarial by design. It deliberately encodes the answer in the prosody for more than half the sister pairs. Any prosody-blind model is structurally guaranteed to fail on those.
  2. Text-only / cascade scoring 8.3% means the falsification controls are doing exactly what they should — they're being punished precisely at the spots where stress is the only cue. That's the experiment working.
  3. 8.3% is the ceiling for any prosody-blind model on this metric, not a floor. The only way to break it is to actually distinguish the two sister clips, which requires hearing the stress.

That's why the primary audio model's 13.5% / 19.5% matters. It isn't "a bit better than chance" — it's the only number on the table that broke the prosody-blind ceiling. The lift comes entirely from clips the text pathways were structurally unable to win.

  • But pair-both-correct tells a different story. PART 7What's Left, and Why Each Piece Matters

7.0 Framing — why pair-consistency isn't the finish line

The pair-consistency result (§6.4) proves the audio model is somewhat sensitive to stress at the argmax (final-answer) level. That's a real finding, but a coarse one. Most of the project's scientific value lives in finer questions that pair-consistency can't answer.

What pair-consistency does not address

  • How big the effect is. 13.5–19.5% pair-both-correct means the model is still failing on 80–87% of pairs. Barely hearing? Or hearing strongly but unable to act on it?
  • Where the signal lives. The model could be 49% confident in the right answer because of stress (a real prosodic shift) — pair-consistency would call that a failure, but the sub-decision logits would show the shift.
  • Whether the effect survives a strict statistical test. Pair-consistency is descriptive — no p-value, no noise-floor comparison.
  • Which component is responsible for the weakness — the audio encoder ("ears") or the language backbone ("brain")?
  • How the effect varies with stress position in the sentence (initial / medial / final).

7.1 The text-rescue probe (next concrete task)

The question this probe answers

So far we know the audio model is somewhat sensitive to stress (the Wilcoxon result) but its final answers are only barely better than chance. A natural follow-up: where in the model does the weakness live? Two suspects:

  • The audio encoder ("ears") — maybe it doesn't pass stress information cleanly into the language model.
  • The language model backbone ("brain") — maybe it receives the stress information fine but doesn't know what to do with it.

You can't open up the model and look. But you can design an experiment that pins down which one is at fault.

The trick

Take the gold transcript and encode the stress in the text by capitalizing the stressed word: "I NEVER said he stole it". Then feed that text to the same text-only LLM you used for the text-only control. Stress is no longer hiding in the audio — it's spelled out in capital letters that any text reader can see.

Now run it. Two possible outcomes, each with a clear architectural interpretation:

Outcome 1 — "Ears failed"

Text-rescue accuracy ≫ text-only baseline → the LLM backbone can use stress when it arrives as text. So the missing ingredient must have been the audio path — stress was in the waveform but didn't survive the trip through the audio encoder into the LM. The bottleneck is the audio encoder.

Outcome 2 — "Brain failed"

Text-rescue ≈ text-only baseline → even handed stress on a silver platter as text, the backbone can't use it. So even a perfect audio encoder wouldn't have helped. The bottleneck is the language model itself.

Why this is worth doing

This is called architectural attribution — narrowing down which component is responsible for an observed deficit. It's the kind of result that turns "the model has a weakness" into "the weakness is here, fix this." Both outcomes are scientifically valuable, and they point to different fixes for whoever builds the next-generation model.
