Project Proposal: Sub-Decision Prosodic Sensitivity — Does Stress Influence Speech-LLM Outputs Below the Final Answer?
Context
The project is inference-only — no training.
1. Motivation and research question
Recent work (the StressTest benchmark, HUJI 2025) has shown that speech-LLMs perform poorly on contrastive stress — utterances with identical words but a different stressed word, and therefore different speaker meaning. That establishes that they fail at the task level. This project asks a finer question:
When a speech-LLM produces the wrong answer on a contrastive-stress item, is the acoustic stress signal absent from its output behavior entirely — or does it shift the output distribution without being strong enough to change the final answer?
This distinction matters. A model whose outputs are wholly unaffected by stress, versus one whose outputs are measurably affected but under-weighted at the decision point, are different diagnoses with different implications for how voice-AI systems should be improved. Top-line accuracy cannot tell them apart; examining the output distribution below the argmax can.
Concrete worked example (illustrative; the final version will quote a verified real item from the StressTest benchmark): the sentence "I didn't say she stole the report," spoken two ways. Audio A — stress on "say" → implication: someone else said it. Audio B — stress on "stole" → implication: she did something with the report other than steal it. The model is asked, forced-choice: "Which implication does the speaker most likely intend?", with the two implications as options. The word string is byte-identical across both audio clips; only the stressed word differs — which is precisely why any text-only system is at chance by construction.
(The StressTest benchmark will be independently verified during Week 1 — existence, format, paired-clip structure, labels, license, and whether it contains multiple takes of identical sentence/stress pairs — and one real item extracted to replace the illustrative example above, before the design is locked.)
2. The core measurement
Rather than reading only the model's final discrete answer, the project inspects the answer distribution.
Each item is forced-choice (the model is prompted to answer with a single token). For each audio condition, a single forward pass yields the output logits; the project extracts the logits of the two competing answer tokens and computes a 2-way softmax over just those two, giving the probability assigned to the correct answer. The quantity of interest is the shift in that probability between the two stress conditions of a pair.
Core hypothesis, stated strictly in terms of observable outputs: If the model's argmax answer is wrong, but the probability of the correct token nonetheless shifts in the stress-appropriate direction across conditions, then stress systematically influences the model's output distribution — even though that influence is not strong enough to change the final answer. The finding is graded: no measurable influence vs. measurable-but-decision-insufficient influence.
What this method can and cannot claim — stated explicitly. This is a black-box, input-output study; it measures facts about outputs only. It does not claim the model "represents," "knows," or "internally encodes" prosody — a token probability is an output statistic, not a measured internal state, and the proposal and write-up deliberately avoid all such mentalistic language. The method also cannot, on its own, localize attenuation to a specific component (audio encoder vs. LLM backbone), because nothing is measured between them. It therefore does not claim "the encoder transmits and the backbone down-weights." It claims only the graded output-level result above. A separate probe (Section 4) provides partial, clearly-bounded evidence on localization.
3. Models and baselines

Primary model under test: Qwen2-Audio-7B-Instruct (native speech-LLM), 4-bit. Tested in full; the project's conclusions stand on it alone.
Second native model (conditional, time-boxed): Qwen2.5-Omni-3B, included as a generalization check only if its inference and logit-extraction pipeline can be brought up within a fixed two-day time-box in Week 2. If tokenizer or prompt-format friction exceeds that box, it is dropped without penalty: the single-model result with full controls is the complete project, and the absence of cross-model evidence is named as an explicit limitation. The contingency is deliberate — the second model strengthens generalizability but must not threaten completion.
Text-only control (falsification only): the text-LLM counterpart, fed the transcript. Because the transcript is byte-identical across a stress pair, it receives an identical input in both conditions and its shift is therefore expected to be ≈0 by construction. Its role is falsification: a near-zero shift confirms the two conditions do not differ in any non-audio way (metadata, ordering, prompt construction); a non-trivial shift would indicate contamination. This control does not define the noise floor — a deterministic text model fed identical input cannot measure how an audio model's output responds to acoustic variation; that is a different kind of noise, measured separately in Section 5.
Whisper cascade: Whisper → text-LLM, included to confirm at the task level that a transcription-based pathway cannot recover stress (its transcript is identical across conditions by construction).

4. Diagnostic probe — partial localization (the text-rescue test)
To get bounded evidence on where the signal is under-used: re-run the items giving the model the stress made explicit in text (an annotation that word X is emphasized). If the model then answers correctly, the LLM backbone is able to use stress when the cue reaches it in usable textual form — implicating the upstream audio pathway as the weak link. If it still fails, the limitation is downstream. This does not deliver a full architectural decomposition, but it is a cheap, inference-only test that meaningfully narrows the explanation, and its conclusions are stated within those limits.
5. Metrics, noise floor, robustness, and statistics
Operational definition. This project operationally defines prosodic stress sensitivity as a measurable, stress-dependent shift in the model's output distribution. Output-distribution shifts are treated as operational evidence of stress sensitivity in model behavior — not as direct measurements of internal representation. Claims are confined to model behavior, and the known artifact sources of shift noise (tokenization quirks, answer-token competition, prompt geometry, numerical instability, and non-prosodic acoustic variance) are addressed by the noise-floor and prompt-robustness controls below — framing and empirical control together.
The central risk of this method is over-interpreting small probability shifts: with enough items, a tiny shift becomes statistically significant. The metrics are designed structurally against this.

Co-headline metric 1 — decision-flip rate. The fraction of stress pairs where changing the stress changes the model's argmax answer in the correct direction. Concrete, needs no interpretation, immune to the tiny-effects problem.
Co-headline metric 2 — stress-induced shift magnitude against an acoustic noise floor. The distribution of correct-token probability shifts caused by changing the stressed word is reported against a properly constructed acoustic noise floor (defined next). Shifts are never reported in isolation.
Acoustic noise floor (the corrected control). Because a deterministic text model cannot measure an audio model's response to acoustic variation, the noise floor is built directly on the audio model, as the distribution of output shifts caused by meaning-preserving acoustic perturbations — changes that alter the waveform but not the words or the stressed word. Option A: if Week 1 verification finds the benchmark contains multiple takes of the same sentence with the same stress pattern, the shift between two such takes defines the floor directly. Option B (default): apply 2–3 meaning-preserving perturbations of graded severity to one condition — e.g. a small gain change (mild), additive low-level noise (mild–moderate), and a small time-stretch or pitch-shift (moderate, and acoustically the closest analogue to what stress varies) — and measure the audio model's shift between clean and perturbed clips. Perturbation magnitudes are capped conservatively and a handful are spot-checked by ear to confirm the perceived stressed word is unchanged; an over-aggressive perturbation that shifts perceived stress would contaminate the floor.
Pre-registered threshold. Before the full run, the threshold is fixed: a stress-induced shift is interpreted as a meaningful prosodic influence only if it exceeds the 95th percentile of the strictest perturbation floor. If the effect clears the strictest floor the result is robust; if it clears only the milder floors, that gradient is reported honestly as the finding. Committed in advance to prevent post-hoc rationalization.
Effect sizes, not just p-values. All shift results reported as magnitudes with effect sizes; significance is secondary to magnitude, consistency, and the decision-flip rate.
Prompt-template robustness check. To rule out that the directional shift is an artifact of one answer-token pair, every item is run under two semantically equivalent answer formats whose answers are carried by different tokens (e.g. Format A → tokens A/B; Format B → a different token set such as 1/2, or the stressed word itself). The check passes if the directional shift survives both formats; partial format-dependence is itself reported as a finding. Run on the primary model only, as a single robustness paragraph — deliberately not expanded into a broader prompt-sensitivity study.
Supporting metric: task-level pair-consistency accuracy (argmax), for comparability with StressTest.
Statistical tests: a paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test on the shifts (paired because identical clips go to each condition; Wilcoxon over a paired t-test because normality of the shift distribution is not assumed), plus a bootstrap 95% CI on the headline magnitudes. Two tests only, both defensible in detail.
Sanity check: confirm argmax over the two extracted logits matches what model.generate() actually produces, on a sample — verifying logit extraction is correct.

6. Execution risk and feasibility
The known hazard is logit extraction: the model's first answer token is often " A" (leading space) — a different, tokenizer-dependent token id from "A". Mitigation: empirically inspect the actual generated token ids per model and per answer format before extraction, prompt for the answer token first, and compare the competing answer tokens against each other rather than reading absolute probabilities. Feasibility is not a concern: 7B in 4-bit is ~5–6GB on a T4; the full run — including both answer formats and the perturbation noise-floor passes — is a few thousand single forward passes, well under an hour.
7. Timeline (6 weeks)
Week 1: verify the StressTest benchmark — format, labels, license, and whether it contains duplicate takes (Option A); extract one real item for the proposal example; 10-item end-to-end smoke test of the logit-extraction pipeline on the primary model. Week 2: finalize extraction on the primary model under both answer formats; build the text-only and cascade pathways; generate and spot-check the meaning-preserving perturbations for the noise floor; time-boxed (2-day) attempt to bring up the second native model — keep or cut per Section 3. Week 3: full run across both answer formats + text-rescue probe + noise-floor passes. Week 4: analysis — stress-induced shift distributions vs. the acoustic noise floor, decision-flip rate, effect sizes, robustness across formats, Wilcoxon, bootstrap. Week 5: figures, error breakdown by stress position. Week 6: write-up and repo. (Two weeks of slack are deliberately built in.)
8. Honest limitations
English-only; bounded to the StressTest stimulus set and its design choices; forced-choice rather than open-ended; all results are observations about output distributions and are not claims about internal model states; the acoustic noise floor is defined by the chosen perturbation set and is a reasonable proxy, not an exhaustive characterization of non-prosodic acoustic variance; the localization probe is partial, not a full architectural decomposition; if the second model is cut, cross-model generalization is untested; results are specific to the tested model versions.
Resume bullet (finding-first, claim-honest)

Probed whether prosodic stress influences open speech-LLM outputs below the final answer — extracted answer-token probabilities to show stress produces a measurable but often decision-insufficient shift in the output distribution, evidence that current models under-use the acoustic channel.
